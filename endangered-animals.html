Success Story

Bald Eagle (Haliaeetus leucocephalus)

USA • Recovered due to strong conservation

Species Details

Common name: Bald eagle. Scientific name: Haliaeetus leucocephalus. It lives near lakes, rivers, and coasts across the USA. The population is estimated at over 300,000 individuals.

Trend & Status

Trend: increasing. Conservation status: IUCN Least Concern. It used to be endangered in the U.S. but was removed from the list in 2007 because numbers recovered.

Analysis

Main threats were pesticides like DDT, habitat loss, and hunting. Eagles are top predators and scavengers, so they help balance the food web and keep ecosystems healthy.

Human Impact

Humans helped by banning DDT, protecting nesting areas, and passing the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act. Conservation and clean water programs made a big difference.

Future / Lessons

Possible future threats include lead poisoning from fishing tackle, habitat loss, and climate change. The lesson is that strong conservation laws can actually bring species back.

Current Crisis

Vaquita (Phocoena sinus)

Gulf of California, Mexico • Critically endangered

Species Details

Common name: Vaquita. Scientific name: Phocoena sinus. It only lives in the northern Gulf of California. Population size is extremely small, around 10 or fewer individuals.

Trend & Status

Trend: decreasing fast. Conservation status: Critically Endangered (IUCN). It is the most endangered marine mammal in the world.

Analysis

The main threat is gillnet fishing, especially illegal nets for totoaba. Vaquitas are part of the marine food web and show the health of the ecosystem and biodiversity.

Human Impact

Humans cause the crisis because gillnets trap vaquitas as bycatch. If vaquitas disappear, the food web loses a species and it shows the ecosystem is being damaged.

Future / Lessons

People can help by enforcing gillnet bans, using safe fishing gear, and supporting local communities to fish sustainably. It’s shocking how quickly a species can vanish.

Extinct

Passenger Pigeon (Ectopistes migratorius)

North America • Extinct in 1914

Species Details

Common name: Passenger pigeon. Scientific name: Ectopistes migratorius. It lived across North America. Population size was once in the billions, but dropped to zero. The last bird, “Martha,” died in 1914.

Trend & Status

Trend: extinct. Conservation status: Extinct (IUCN). The species disappeared completely from the wild, and the last captive bird died in 1914.

Analysis

Main causes were huge commercial hunting and habitat loss from deforestation. The pigeons helped with seed dispersal and shaped forest ecosystems, so their loss affected biodiversity.

Human Impact

Humans overhunted them for food and destroyed nesting forests. This is a clear example of how fast a species can be wiped out when there are no limits or protection.

Future / Lessons

The lesson is to protect habitat early and control hunting before a species collapses. One thing I learned is that “common” animals can still go extinct.
